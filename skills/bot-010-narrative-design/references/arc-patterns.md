# Arc Patterns

Ready-made narrative arcs by deck type. Pick the one that matches the audience, then
adapt the section names and slide count to the topic. These generalize the
section-by-section journey of the upstream `vibe-to-agentic-framework` skill into
topic-agnostic templates.

Every arc shares the same frame:

- **Opening** — 1–2 slides. A title slide, then a hook/context slide. No progress weight.
- **Body** — 2–5 named sections. Each builds on the sections before it.
- **Closing** — 1 slide. The resolution, recommendation, or call to action.
- **Appendix** — optional. Reference material; no progress weight.

---

## Pitch

For investors, buyers, or decision-makers you need to persuade and ask something of.

| # | Section | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| Opening | Title + Problem | Name the deck; make the audience feel the problem and its cost. |
| 1 | Solution | The product/idea, framed as the answer to the problem just shown. |
| 2 | How it works | One concrete walk-through using the running example. |
| 3 | Traction / proof | Evidence it works — usage, results, testimonials, milestones. |
| 4 | Market / opportunity | Why this is big and why now. |
| 5 | Team (optional) | Why this group can execute. |
| Closing | The ask | The specific request — investment, sale, partnership — and next step. |

Typical length 10–14 slides. Progress style: `percentage` or `none`. No journey levels —
a pitch is not a learning journey.

## Tutorial / course

For learners who finish the deck more capable than they started.

| # | Section | Journey level |
|---|---|---|
| Opening | Title + What you'll learn | — |
| 1 | Prerequisites / setup | (level 1 starts at the first lesson, not here) |
| 2 | Foundations | Level 1 — e.g. "Beginner" |
| 3 | Core skills | Level 2 — e.g. "Intermediate" |
| 4 | Advanced / putting it together | Level 3 — e.g. "Confident" |
| Closing | Recap + where to go next | — |

Typical length 12–20 slides. Progress style: `journey-levels` — the audience watches
levels accumulate. Name 3–4 ordered levels and attach each to a body section divider.
Carry one sample project through every lesson as the running example.

## Internal update

For a team or leadership who need status and decisions.

| # | Section | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| Opening | Title + TL;DR | The headline outcome in one slide. |
| 1 | Where we are | Status against the plan — shipped, in progress. |
| 2 | What we learned | Findings, metrics, surprises. |
| 3 | Risks / blockers | What threatens the next phase. |
| Closing | Next steps + asks | What happens next and what is needed from the room. |

Typical length 7–11 slides. Progress style: `percentage` or `none`.

---

## Overview / explainer

The default when the audience is mixed or unstated — explain an idea, product, or
domain to a general audience.

| # | Section | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| Opening | Title + Why this matters | Hook: the question or tension the deck resolves. |
| 1 | The big idea | The core concept in one clear statement. |
| 2 | How it works | Break the idea into 2–3 parts, each its own slide. |
| 3 | In practice | The running example, start to finish. |
| 4 | Implications (optional) | What changes because of this. |
| Closing | Takeaway | The one thing to remember, plus a next step. |

Typical length 8–14 slides. Progress style: `percentage` (default).

---

## Adapting a pattern

- **Merge or split sections** to fit the topic — a simple topic may need only 2 body
  sections, a rich one up to 5. Never exceed 5 body sections; beyond that the audience
  loses the thread.
- **Rename sections** to the topic's own vocabulary — "Traction" becomes "Results so
  far", "Foundations" becomes "Git's three areas". Keep the *role* each section plays.
- **One core idea per content slide.** When a section has more than ~4 slides,
  consider whether it is really two sections.
- **Never forward-reference.** If section 3 needs a term, section 1 or 2 must have
  introduced it.
