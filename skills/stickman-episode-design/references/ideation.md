# Ideation — territories and the concretization method

Depth reference for `bot-013-episode-design`. Load when the topic is vague, abstract,
branded, or missing detail. The skill never asks the user — it concretizes, commits, and
records the assumption.

## 1. The home territories

The format's proven ideation space is relatable everyday life. Six territories dominate
the genre (community-validated); each lists ready concrete scenarios to adapt:

| territory | what it covers | example concrete scenarios |
|---|---|---|
| **Life hacks** | shortcuts that backfire or barely work | folding a fitted sheet "the easy way"; the one-pot recipe that uses every pot; speed-packing a suitcase by sitting on it |
| **Psychology** | the gap between intention and behavior | rearranging the desk instead of starting work; rehearsing a confrontation that never happens; checking the fridge again knowing it is empty |
| **Money** | small spending and saving absurdities | cancelling one subscription, gaining three; carrying the basket so it "doesn't count" as a big shop; the free-shipping threshold top-up item |
| **Discipline** | routines, willpower, and their decay | the 5 a.m. routine that survives one day; snoozing the alarm until the day is gone; gym bag packed, never leaves the hallway |
| **Habits** | tiny loops that own us | one-more-episode at 2 a.m.; watering the one plant after it died; the chair that collects clothes |
| **Modern digital life** | screens vs. the physical world | doom-scrolling while the bath overflows; video call frozen mid-gesture; following GPS into an obvious dead end |

Stay inside (or adjacent to) these — they are what the audience subscribes for, and they
are inherently domestic, prop-rich, and silent-comedy friendly.

## 2. The concretization method (vague topic → filmable scenario)

A topic is *concrete enough* when you can already see the final still. Test: does it name
(or directly imply) a PLACE, a PROP, and a PRESSURE? "Procrastination" fails all three;
"snoozing the alarm until the day is gone" gives bedroom / phone / daylight running out.

When the topic fails the test:

1. **Map it to a territory.** "life" → discipline or habits; "stress" → psychology;
   "being broke" → money.
2. **Pick ONE everyday scenario** from that territory — one place, one physical prop the
   joke can hang on, one pressure (time, space, a growing pile). Resist anthologies: one
   episode = one scenario, not three vignettes.
3. **Write the commitment as one line** ("a stickman keeps snoozing his alarm until the
   day is gone") and use it as the logline seed.
4. **Record the assumption** — a bullet in the plan's `## Notes` ("vague topic 'life'
   concretized to ...") AND a line in state.md's Decisions log. Headless runs must leave
   an audit trail instead of asking.

Worked mappings:

| user topic | territory | committed scenario |
|---|---|---|
| "life" | habits | the chair that collects clothes until it becomes the wardrobe |
| "discipline" | discipline | snoozing the alarm until the day is gone (see worked example B) |
| "money stuff" | money | adding a cheap item to reach free shipping, three times |
| "make something about phones" | modern digital life | doom-scrolling while the bath slowly overflows |
| "cooking" | life hacks | the one-pan recipe that somehow uses every dish in the kitchen |

## 3. Safety and IP scrub (apply while concretizing)

These rewrites happen silently, with a `## Notes` bullet — never a user question:

- **Brands → generic stand-ins.** "IKEA episode" → a generic flat-pack wardrobe box (the
  project slug may echo the brief; scene blocks stay generic). Labels in frame use plain
  words ("FRAGILE", "TASK"), never marks or logos.
- **Real people / characters → the stickman.** Celebrity or franchise premises become
  the stickman in an analogous everyday situation, or get re-anchored to the nearest
  territory scenario.
- **Family-friendly, advertiser-safe.** Slapstick at most; no violence, no humiliation
  punching down, no adult themes. The humor is observational — the viewer laughs because
  it is THEM, not because someone gets hurt.

## 4. Picking the punchline

When the user supplies no punchline line, write one: ≤10 words, deadpan, present tense,
the caption-card voice of the episode ("Some assembly required." / "Just five more
minutes."). It should restate the joke, not explain it. Prefer lines that still read
correctly when the Short loops back to beat 1.
