# Copywriting

## Purpose

Write, rewrite, or improve marketing copy for any page -- homepage, landing pages, pricing pages, feature pages, about pages, or product pages. Use this skill when creating new copy from scratch or substantially rewriting existing copy. For editing and polishing existing copy, use `skills/bot-001-copy-editing/SKILL.md` after your draft is complete.

## Instructions

### Step 1: Gather Context Before Writing

Collect or ask for this information before writing a single word:

**Page Purpose:**
- What type of page? (homepage, landing page, pricing, feature, about)
- What is the ONE primary action visitors should take?

**Audience:**
- Who is the ideal customer?
- What problem are they trying to solve?
- What objections or hesitations do they have?
- What language do they use to describe their problem? (Pull from reviews, support tickets, interviews)

**Product/Offer:**
- What are you selling or offering?
- What makes it different from alternatives?
- What is the key transformation or outcome?
- What proof points exist? (numbers, testimonials, case studies)

**Traffic Context:**
- Where is traffic coming from? (ads, organic, email, social)
- What do visitors already know before arriving?

### Step 2: Apply Core Writing Principles

**Clarity Over Cleverness.** If you must choose between clear and creative, choose clear. Every time.

**Benefits Over Features.** Features describe what it does. Benefits describe what that means for the customer. Always bridge to benefits.

**Specificity Over Vagueness.**
- Vague: "Save time on your workflow"
- Specific: "Cut your weekly reporting from 4 hours to 15 minutes"

**Customer Language Over Company Language.** Use words your customers actually use. Mirror voice-of-customer from reviews, interviews, and support tickets.

**One Idea Per Section.** Each section should advance one argument. Build a logical flow down the page.

### Step 3: Follow Writing Style Rules

1. **Simple over complex** -- "Use" not "utilize," "help" not "facilitate"
2. **Specific over vague** -- Avoid "streamline," "optimize," "innovative"
3. **Active over passive** -- "We generate reports" not "Reports are generated"
4. **Confident over qualified** -- Remove "almost," "very," "really"
5. **Show over tell** -- Describe the outcome instead of using adverbs
6. **Honest over sensational** -- Never fabricate statistics or testimonials

**Quick quality check before delivering:**
- Any jargon that could confuse outsiders?
- Any sentences trying to do too much?
- Any passive voice constructions?
- Any exclamation points? (Remove them.)
- Any marketing buzzwords without substance?

For thorough line-by-line review, use `skills/bot-001-copy-editing/SKILL.md` after your draft.

### Step 4: Structure the Page

#### Above the Fold

**Headline** -- Your single most important message. Communicate core value proposition. Specific beats generic.

**Headline formulas:**

*Outcome-Focused:*
- "{Achieve desirable outcome} without {pain point}" -- e.g., "Understand how users experience your site without drowning in numbers"
- "{Achieve desirable outcome} by {how product makes it possible}" -- e.g., "Generate more leads by seeing which companies visit your site"
- "Turn {input} into {outcome}" -- e.g., "Turn your hard-earned sales into repeat customers"
- "[Achieve outcome] in [timeframe]" -- e.g., "Get your tax refund in 10 days"

*Problem-Focused:*
- "Never {unpleasant event} again" -- e.g., "Never miss a sales opportunity again"
- "{Question highlighting the main pain point}" -- e.g., "Hate returning stuff to Amazon?"
- "Stop [pain]. Start [pleasure]." -- e.g., "Stop chasing invoices. Start getting paid on time."

*Audience-Focused:*
- "{Key feature/product type} for {target audience}" -- e.g., "Advanced analytics for Shopify e-commerce"
- "{Key feature} for {audience} to {what it is used for}" -- e.g., "An online whiteboard for teams to ideate and brainstorm together"
- "You don't have to {skills or resources} to {achieve outcome}" -- e.g., "With Ahrefs, you don't have to be an SEO pro to rank higher and get more traffic"

*Differentiation-Focused:*
- "The {opposite of usual process} way to {achieve outcome}" -- e.g., "The easiest way to turn your passion into income"
- "The [category] that [key differentiator]" -- e.g., "The CRM that updates itself"

*Proof-Focused:*
- "[Number] [people] use [product] to [outcome]" -- e.g., "50,000 marketers use Drip to send better emails"

*Additional Formulas:*
- "Finally, {category} that {benefit}" -- e.g., "Finally, accounting software that doesn't suck"
- "What if you could {desirable outcome}?" -- e.g., "What if you could close deals 30% faster?"
- "Everything you need to {outcome}" -- e.g., "Everything you need to launch your course"
- "The {adjective} {category} built for {audience}" -- e.g., "The lightweight CRM built for startups"
- "Get {benefit} from your {thing}" -- e.g., "Get more revenue from your existing traffic"

**Subheadline** -- Expands on headline. Adds specificity. 1-2 sentences max.

**Primary CTA** -- Action-oriented button text. Communicate what they get: "Start Free Trial" beats "Sign Up."

#### Core Page Sections

| Section | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| Social Proof | Build credibility (logos, stats, testimonials) |
| Problem/Pain | Show you understand their situation |
| Solution/Benefits | Connect to outcomes (3-5 key benefits) |
| How It Works | Reduce perceived complexity (3-4 steps) |
| Objection Handling | FAQ, comparisons, guarantees |
| Final CTA | Recap value, repeat CTA, risk reversal |

#### Supporting Sections

- **Testimonials** -- Full quotes with names, roles, companies. Specific results over vague praise. Avoid "Great product!" -- use "increased conversions by 32%."
- **Case Studies** -- Problem, Solution, Results format with specific metrics.
- **Use Cases** -- Different ways product is used. Helps visitors self-identify.
- **Personas / "Built For" Sections** -- Explicitly call out target audience. Addresses "Is this for me?"
- **FAQ** -- Address common objections. Good for SEO. 5-10 most common questions.
- **Comparison** -- vs. competitors or vs. status quo (spreadsheets, manual processes).
- **Integrations** -- Logos of tools you connect with. "Works with your stack."
- **Guarantee / Risk Reversal** -- Money-back guarantee, free trial terms, "Cancel anytime."

#### Page Structure Templates

**Varied, Engaging Page (recommended):**
1. Hero with clear value prop
2. Social proof bar (logos or stats)
3. Problem/pain section
4. How it works (3 steps)
5. Key benefits (2-3, not 10)
6. Testimonial
7. Use cases or personas
8. Comparison to alternatives
9. Case study snippet
10. FAQ
11. Final CTA with guarantee

**Compact Landing Page (for ad traffic):**
1. Hero (headline, subhead, CTA, image)
2. Social proof bar
3. 3 key benefits with icons
4. Testimonial
5. How it works (3 steps)
6. Final CTA with guarantee

**Enterprise/B2B Landing Page:**
1. Hero (outcome-focused headline)
2. Logo bar (recognizable companies)
3. Problem section (business pain)
4. Solution overview
5. Use cases by role/department
6. Security/compliance section
7. Integration logos
8. Case study with metrics
9. ROI/value section
10. Contact/demo CTA

**Product Launch Page:**
1. Hero with launch announcement
2. Video demo or walkthrough
3. Feature highlights (3-5)
4. Before/after comparison
5. Early testimonials
6. Launch pricing or early access offer
7. CTA with urgency

### Step 5: Write Strong CTAs

**Weak CTAs to avoid:** Submit, Sign Up, Learn More, Click Here, Get Started

**Strong CTA formula:** [Action Verb] + [What They Get] + [Qualifier if needed]

**Examples:**
- "Start My Free Trial"
- "Get the Complete Checklist"
- "See Pricing for My Team"
- "Create Your First [Thing]"
- "Download the Guide"

### Step 6: Use Best Practices

**Be Direct.** Get to the point. Do not bury the value in qualifications.
- Bad: "Slack lets you share files instantly, from documents to images, directly in your conversations"
- Good: "Need to share a screenshot? Send as many documents, images, and audio files as your heart desires."

**Use Rhetorical Questions.** Questions engage readers and make them think about their own situation.
- "Hate returning stuff to Amazon?"
- "Tired of chasing approvals?"

**Use Analogies When Helpful.** Analogies make abstract concepts concrete and memorable.

**Use Humor Sparingly.** Puns and wit make copy memorable, but only if it fits the brand and does not undermine clarity.

### Step 7: Use Natural Transitions

Use these phrases to guide readers between sections:

**Previewing content:** "Here's what we'll cover..." / "This guide walks you through..." / "Let's break this down step by step."

**Introducing a new topic:** "When it comes to X..." / "Now let's talk about X." / "Another key factor is..."

**Indicating contrast:** "However,..." / "That said,..." / "On the flip side,..."

**Indicating cause and effect:** "This means..." / "That's why..." / "Here's what happens:..."

**Giving examples:** "For example,..." / "Take X, for instance." / "Say you want to..."

**Emphasizing key points:** "Here's the key takeaway:..." / "What matters most is..." / "The bottom line?..."

**Transitions to AVOID (AI tells):**
- "That being said,..."
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "In today's digital landscape,..."
- "Let's delve into..."
- "At its core,..."

### Step 8: Adapt for Page Type

**Homepage** -- Serve multiple audiences without being generic. Lead with broadest value proposition. Provide clear paths for different visitor intents.

**Landing Page** -- Single message, single CTA. Match headline to ad or traffic source. Complete argument on one page.

**Pricing Page** -- Help visitors choose the right plan. Address "which is right for me?" anxiety. Make recommended plan obvious. See also: `skills/bot-001-pricing-strategy/SKILL.md`.

**Feature Page** -- Connect feature to benefit to outcome. Show use cases and examples. Clear path to try or buy.

**About Page** -- Tell the story of why you exist. Connect mission to customer benefit. Still include a CTA.

### Step 9: Set Voice and Tone

Before writing, establish:

**Formality level:** Casual/conversational, professional but friendly, or formal/enterprise.

**Brand personality:** Playful or serious? Bold or understated? Technical or accessible?

Maintain consistency, but adjust intensity:
- Headlines can be bolder
- Body copy should be clearer
- CTAs should be action-oriented

### Section Writing Tips

**Problem Section** -- Start with: "You know the feeling..." / "If you're like most [role]..." / "Every day, [audience] struggles with..." Then describe the specific frustration, time/money wasted, and impact on their work.

**Benefits Section** -- For each benefit include: a headline (the outcome they get), body (how it works, 1-2 sentences), and proof (number, testimonial, or example).

**How It Works Section** -- Each step should be numbered, use a simple verb ("Connect," "Set up," "Get"), and be outcome-oriented. Example: 1. Connect your tools (takes 2 minutes) 2. Set your preferences 3. Get automated reports every Monday.

**Testimonial Selection** -- Best testimonials include specific results ("increased conversions by 32%"), before/after context ("We used to spend hours..."), and role + company for credibility.

## Inputs

- Page type and purpose
- Target audience description
- Product or offer details
- Key differentiators
- Available proof points (testimonials, case studies, numbers)
- Traffic source (where visitors come from)
- Brand voice guidelines (if they exist)
- Existing copy to improve (if rewriting)

## Outputs

When writing copy, deliver:

1. **Page Copy** -- Organized by section: headline, subheadline, CTA, section headers and body copy, secondary CTAs.
2. **Annotations** -- For key elements, explain why you made this choice and what principle it applies.
3. **Alternatives** -- For headlines and CTAs, provide 2-3 options with rationale:
   - Option A: [copy] -- [rationale]
   - Option B: [copy] -- [rationale]
4. **Meta Content** (if relevant) -- Page title (for SEO) and meta description.

Save outputs to `work/copy/` with descriptive filenames.

## Examples

**Example 1: SaaS Landing Page Headline Options**

Context: Project management tool for remote teams, traffic from Google Ads targeting "remote project management."

- Option A: "Keep your remote team aligned without the endless status meetings" -- Outcome-focused, addresses the pain of too many meetings
- Option B: "The project management tool built for teams that don't share an office" -- Audience-focused, immediately qualifies the reader
- Option C: "12,000 remote teams use Acme to ship projects on time" -- Proof-focused, builds immediate credibility

**Example 2: Feature Page CTA**

Context: Analytics dashboard feature for a marketing tool.

- Weak: "Learn More"
- Better: "See Your Dashboard"
- Best: "See Where Your Traffic Really Comes From" -- Specific benefit, creates curiosity, action-oriented

## Quality Criteria

- Headline communicates the core value proposition in customer language
- Every feature mentioned is connected to a benefit
- Copy uses specific numbers, timeframes, or examples (not vague claims)
- CTA tells the user what they will get, not just what to do
- Page structure follows a logical flow (not just a feature list)
- Voice and tone are consistent throughout
- No jargon, buzzwords, or passive voice
- At least 2-3 headline alternatives are provided with rationale
- Copy passes the "So what?" test -- every claim answers "why should I care?"
